# Citizenship Valley Curriculum Map

This map turns the GCSE Citizenship course into game regions. It uses the AQA GCSE Citizenship Studies structure as the main spine, with overlap for the wider GCSE subject content used across English exam boards.

## World Structure

| Game Region | GCSE Area | Core Purpose |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Prologue: Citizenship Village | Citizenship skills, processes and methods | Teach movement, dialogue, evidence, viewpoints, judgement, and active citizenship habits. |
| Modern Britain Borough | Life in modern Britain | Identity, diversity, British values, media, free press, migration, UK in the world. |
| Rights & Law Quarter | Rights and responsibilities | Rights, duties, law, justice system, civil/criminal law, police, courts, human rights. |
| Democracy Capital | Politics and participation | Democracy, Parliament, government, elections, parties, voting systems, representation, devolution. |
| Participation Harbour | Citizen voice and public action | Campaigns, pressure groups, parties, petitions, media, protest, volunteering, holding power to account. |
| Action Workshop | Active citizenship | Research, planning, action, impact, evaluation, reflection, exam write-up. |
| Exam Hall | Assessment practice | Source analysis, explain questions, compare questions, judgement paragraphs, long answers. |

## World Fantasy And Region Types

The game world should feel like a small country the player gradually understands. Each region is a different kind of place, matching the subject matter and giving the player a reason to travel.

### 1. Citizenship Village

Location Type: cosy tutorial town.

Visual Mood:

- A friendly market village with a town hall, library, small court, park, river, noticeboard, and schoolhouse.
- Warm greens, stone paths, wooden signs, soft lamps, banners, and simple homes.
- This is the "Stardew Valley" comfort zone where basic systems are introduced.

Why It Fits:

- Citizenship starts with everyday community life.
- The player learns that politics, law, rights, and participation are not abstract; they affect ordinary places.

How The Player Arrives:

- The player is a new student sent to Citizenship Valley for a "Civic Trial".
- Mayor Ada gives them a Civic Notebook and asks them to earn regional badges before the GCSE Exam Gate opens.

Exit To Next Region:

- After completing the village tutorial, the railway station opens.
- The first train goes to Modern Britain Borough.

### 2. Modern Britain Borough

Location Type: stylised London-inspired city district.

Visual Mood:

- A dense, colourful city with high streets, flats, museums, embassies, bus stops, news kiosks, murals, community centres, and a riverside skyline.
- Not a literal London copy, but clearly inspired by a multicultural UK city.
- Areas within it can represent identity, media, migration, shared values, and the UK in the world.

Sub-Areas:

- Identity Square: flags, murals, family-history stalls, community groups.
- Free Press Printworks: newspaper office, podcast booth, camera crew, social media notice wall.
- Migration Museum: interactive exhibits about movement, settlement, and community change.
- Embassy Row: international organisations and global citizenship.
- Community Garden: shared values, mutual respect, local cooperation.

Why It Fits:

- "Life in modern Britain" is about identity, diversity, media, rights, and the UK's place in the world.
- A city naturally lets the player meet different communities, sources, and viewpoints.

How The Player Gets There:

- Train from Citizenship Village.
- The train ticket is the "Identity Pass", earned by proving the player can distinguish fact, opinion, and viewpoint.

Exit To Next Region:

- The player unlocks a press badge and a city travel card.
- The Underground line leads to Rights & Law Quarter.

### 3. Rights & Law Quarter

Location Type: old legal district with courts, archives, and shadowy alley cases.

Visual Mood:

- Cobblestone streets, courthouse steps, police station, rights archive, legal aid office, youth justice centre, and equality bridge.
- Slightly more serious atmosphere: rain, lamplight, parchment notices, court seals, blue police lamps.
- Still safe for a school game, but with mystery-investigation energy.

Sub-Areas:

- Rule of Law Court: trials, evidence, fair process.
- Rights Archive: Human Rights Act, freedoms, equality.
- Police Station: powers, safeguards, accountability.
- Civil Disputes Lane: contracts, family disputes, consumer issues.
- Youth Justice Centre: sentencing, rehabilitation, responsibility.
- Equality Bridge: discrimination and fairness.

Why It Fits:

- Law is about rules, evidence, disputes, fairness, and limits on power.
- The visual language can feel like detective work: find facts, test claims, make fair judgements.

How The Player Gets There:

- Underground from Modern Britain Borough.
- A ticket inspector asks a source-reliability question before allowing entry.

Exit To Next Region:

- After earning the Rights Defender badge, the courthouse clock opens a lift to the hilltop Parliament district.
- This leads to Democracy Capital.

### 4. Democracy Capital

Location Type: grand hilltop capital with Parliament, election arenas, and guild halls.

Visual Mood:

- A dramatic civic capital: parliament towers, debating chambers, election boards, party guild halls, devolution gates, manifesto printers.
- More formal and strategic, with banners, maps, ballot boxes, debating stages, and clockwork law machines.
- This should feel like the "main city" of power.

Sub-Areas:

- Parliament Keep: MPs, Lords, bills, scrutiny.
- Election Arena: voting systems, constituencies, campaigns.
- Party Guildhall: parties, manifestos, political values.
- Devolution Gate: Scotland, Wales, Northern Ireland, local/national power.
- Constitution Library: uncodified constitution, monarchy, checks and balances.
- Digital Democracy Tower: online participation and modern engagement.

Why It Fits:

- Politics and participation need a sense of institutions and power.
- A capital city makes the player feel they are entering the machinery of democracy.

How The Player Gets There:

- Clock-lift from the courthouse or a funicular railway up the civic hill.
- The player must carry the "Rule of Law Seal", proving they understand that power is limited by law.

Exit To Next Region:

- Completing election and Parliament quests unlocks the Campaign Ferry.
- It sails downriver to Participation Harbour.

### 5. Participation Harbour

Location Type: working harbour and festival protest waterfront.

Visual Mood:

- Docks, boats, campaign stalls, union halls, charity warehouses, petition boards, speaker corners, social media signal masts.
- Lively and noisy: banners, posters, street music, volunteers, public meetings.
- A little like an RPG port town where information and people are always moving.

Sub-Areas:

- Petition Pier: petition design and public support.
- Campaign Market: campaign materials, slogans, target audiences.
- Protest Green: lawful protest, rights and responsibilities.
- Volunteer Dock: charities, community action, mutual aid.
- Pressure Group Offices: lobbying, influence, ethics.
- Signal Tower: media campaigns and social networks.

Why It Fits:

- Participation is action, movement, persuasion, and public voice.
- A harbour is a strong metaphor: ideas arrive, spread, and leave for wider society.

How The Player Gets There:

- Campaign Ferry from Democracy Capital.
- On arrival, Priya gives the player a campaign satchel.

Exit To Next Region:

- The player builds a campaign boat by completing participation quests.
- The boat travels to Action Workshop, a converted island factory where ideas become projects.

### 6. Action Workshop

Location Type: converted island workshop / makerspace.

Visual Mood:

- A practical project island with research labs, planning boards, survey booths, printing presses, meeting rooms, event stage, and impact observatory.
- More hands-on and modular: desks, charts, sticky notes, maps, tool racks, project boards.
- This is where RPG crafting meets active citizenship.

Sub-Areas:

- Research Lab: primary and secondary evidence.
- Survey Street: questionnaires and interviews.
- Planning Board: aims, targets, methods.
- Action Stage: public action and campaign delivery.
- Impact Observatory: measuring outcomes.
- Reflection Room: evaluation and improvements.

Why It Fits:

- Active citizenship is not only knowledge; it is project work.
- The workshop metaphor makes research, planning, action, and evaluation feel like crafting.

How The Player Gets There:

- Campaign boat from Participation Harbour.
- The player must bring at least three evidence items from earlier regions: a source, a viewpoint, and a legal/democratic concept.

Exit To Next Region:

- Completing the active citizenship project powers a lighthouse.
- Its beam reveals the Exam Hall: a castle that only appears when the player has enough badges.

### 7. Exam Hall

Location Type: magical exam castle / academy fortress.

Visual Mood:

- A strange castle-school hybrid: towers shaped like pencils, source-scroll libraries, timed challenge rooms, debate arena, paragraph forge, boss-question hall.
- More magical and intense than previous regions, but not scary.
- The castle rearranges itself by command word: describe, explain, analyse, evaluate.

Sub-Areas:

- Command Word Corridor: identify, describe, explain, analyse, evaluate.
- Source Analysis Library: reliability, usefulness, provenance.
- Paragraph Forge: PEEL paragraphs and evidence.
- Debate Arena: balanced arguments and judgement.
- Timed Trial Tower: timed exam practice.
- Final Paper Gate: mixed-topic boss challenge.

Why It Fits:

- Exams feel like a final trial.
- A magical castle makes assessment practice feel special instead of like a worksheet.

How The Player Gets There:

- Lighthouse beam from Action Workshop opens a bridge through the fog.
- The player must have the six regional mastery badges to enter the Final Paper Gate.

Endgame Loop:

- The player can replay castle rooms for timed practice.
- Rooms remix questions from all previous regions.
- Better answers upgrade equipment skins and unlock higher grade titles.

## Travel System

The player should not use a plain menu at first. Travel should feel diegetic:

| From | To | Travel Method | Unlock |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Citizenship Village | Modern Britain Borough | Train | Complete tutorial badge. |
| Modern Britain Borough | Rights & Law Quarter | Underground line | Pass a source-reliability check. |
| Rights & Law Quarter | Democracy Capital | Courthouse clock-lift / funicular | Earn Rights Defender badge. |
| Democracy Capital | Participation Harbour | Campaign Ferry | Complete Parliament and election quests. |
| Participation Harbour | Action Workshop | Campaign boat | Build a campaign plan. |
| Action Workshop | Exam Hall | Lighthouse bridge | Complete active citizenship project. |

After the player visits a region once, fast travel can unlock through the Civic Notebook.

## World Logic

- The whole map is connected by the River Civic.
- The river starts in Citizenship Village, passes through the city, law quarter, capital, harbour, workshop island, and finally reaches the fog around Exam Hall.
- This makes the journey feel continuous: local community -> modern society -> law -> power -> participation -> action -> exam mastery.
- Each region should have a different dominant activity:
  - Village: learn basics.
  - Borough: investigate identity and media.
  - Law Quarter: solve cases.
  - Capital: debate and vote.
  - Harbour: campaign and persuade.
  - Workshop: build a project.
  - Exam Hall: prove mastery.

## Prologue: Citizenship Village

Role: tutorial and early confidence zone.

Topics:

- What citizenship means.
- Rights, responsibilities, democracy, justice, identity, participation.
- How to collect evidence and listen to different views.
- Difference between opinion, fact, evidence, and judgement.

NPCs:

- Mayor Ada: democracy basics.
- Sam the Librarian: evidence and reliable sources.
- Priya the Campaigner: participation basics.
- Justice Rowan: law and fairness.
- Councillor Noor: local community action.

Quest Types:

- Fetch an explanation from another NPC.
- Choose the strongest evidence.
- Sort statements into fact, opinion, and judgement.
- Learn the inventory/equipment system.

Rewards:

- Starter notebook.
- Revision tea.
- Basic school outfit.
- First badge: Informed Citizen.

## Modern Britain Borough

Role: social identity and media region.

Core Topics:

- Principles and values underpinning British society.
- Human, moral, legal and political rights.
- Individual, group, national, and global identity.
- The UK as England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland.
- Diversity, migration, mutual respect, and shared values.
- Traditional media, new media, free press, accuracy, privacy, dignity.
- Press regulation and censorship.
- UK role in international organisations, including UN, NATO, Council of Europe, Commonwealth, WTO, and the continuing impact of former EU membership.
- NGOs and humanitarian crises.

Locations:

- Identity Square: multiple identities and diversity.
- Free Press Printworks: media, regulation, privacy, public interest.
- Migration Museum: population change and community impact.
- World Embassy Row: UK international organisations.
- Community Garden: mutual respect and shared values.

NPCs:

- Editor Vale: free press and public interest.
- Historian Iona: UK nations and identity.
- Aid Worker Mina: NGOs and humanitarian crises.
- Data Clerk Omar: migration and population change.
- Community Elder Grace: respect and cohesion.

Quest Examples:

- Check whether a newspaper claim is accurate before it spreads.
- Interview residents with different identities and build a “community map”.
- Find which international organisation handles a crisis.
- Decide whether privacy or public interest is stronger in a media case.
- Compare how migration can create opportunities and challenges.

Exam Skills:

- Explain two ways identity can be shaped.
- Analyse media influence.
- Evaluate free press versus privacy.
- Use examples to support a point.

## Rights & Law Quarter

Role: legal system, justice, rights and responsibilities.

Core Topics:

- Nature of law and why laws are needed.
- Rule of law.
- Civil law and criminal law.
- Legal rights and responsibilities.
- Human Rights Act and rights/freedoms.
- Equality and discrimination.
- Police powers and citizen rights.
- Courts and tribunals.
- Sentencing aims and youth justice.
- Citizens in the legal system, including jury service.
- Balancing rights, public safety, and responsibilities.

Locations:

- Rule of Law Court.
- Rights Archive.
- Police Station.
- Civil Disputes Lane.
- Youth Justice Centre.
- Equality Bridge.

NPCs:

- Justice Rowan: courts and rule of law.
- Sergeant Blake: police powers and accountability.
- Advocate Farah: human rights and equality.
- Mediator Chen: civil disputes.
- Youth Worker Ellis: youth justice and rehabilitation.

Quest Examples:

- Decide whether a case is civil or criminal.
- Match police powers with citizen safeguards.
- Build a fair sentence using aims: punishment, deterrence, rehabilitation, protection, reparation.
- Defend a right while recognising a responsibility.
- Investigate a discrimination case and choose the lawful response.

Exam Skills:

- Define rule of law.
- Distinguish civil and criminal law.
- Explain rights and responsibilities together.
- Evaluate whether a legal response is fair.

## Democracy Capital

Role: political power and formal participation.

Core Topics:

- Democracy and democratic values.
- Where political power resides in the UK.
- UK constitution and constitutional principles.
- Parliament, government, Prime Minister, Cabinet.
- House of Commons and House of Lords.
- Making and scrutinising laws.
- Holding government to account.
- Elections, voting, constituencies, representatives.
- Political parties and manifestos.
- Electoral systems, including First Past the Post and alternatives.
- Devolution and local/national government.
- Monarchy and ceremonial roles.
- Comparing democratic and non-democratic systems.
- Digital democracy and engagement.

Locations:

- Parliament Keep.
- Election Arena.
- Party Guildhall.
- Devolution Gate.
- Constitution Library.
- Digital Democracy Tower.

NPCs:

- Speaker Lark: Parliament procedure.
- MP Rivers: representation and constituencies.
- Campaign Manager Sol: parties and manifestos.
- Returning Officer June: elections and voting systems.
- Devolution Herald Ewan: devolved power.

Quest Examples:

- Track a bill from proposal to law.
- Run a mock election and compare voting systems.
- Match powers to Parliament, government, local council, and devolved administrations.
- Analyse a manifesto and identify political priorities.
- Compare a democratic country with a non-democratic system.

Exam Skills:

- Explain how Parliament holds government to account.
- Compare voting systems.
- Analyse strengths and weaknesses of representative democracy.
- Use political vocabulary accurately.

## Participation Harbour

Role: informal participation, campaigns, public voice.

Core Topics:

- Opportunities and barriers to participation.
- Voting, joining parties, standing for election.
- Interest groups, pressure groups, trade unions, charities, voluntary groups.
- Petitions, lobbying, advocacy, campaigning, demonstrations.
- Volunteering and community action.
- Holding those in power to account.
- Media and social media in campaigns.
- Advantages and disadvantages of different forms of participation.

Locations:

- Petition Pier.
- Campaign Market.
- Protest Green.
- Volunteer Dock.
- Pressure Group Offices.
- Social Media Signal Tower.

NPCs:

- Priya the Campaigner: strategy and petitions.
- Union Rep Morgan: collective action.
- Charity Lead Amina: volunteering.
- Lobbyist Pax: influence and ethics.
- Digital Moderator Rae: online participation.

Quest Examples:

- Choose the best participation method for different issues.
- Build a petition with a clear target and evidence.
- Identify barriers to participation and suggest solutions.
- Compare a pressure group and a political party.
- Decide whether a protest is effective, lawful, and ethical.

Exam Skills:

- Explain advantages and disadvantages.
- Compare forms of participation.
- Evaluate campaign effectiveness.
- Link methods to real-world impact.

## Action Workshop

Role: active citizenship project zone.

Core Topics:

- Identify an issue.
- Research with primary and secondary evidence.
- Choose aims and target audience.
- Plan action.
- Carry out action.
- Assess impact.
- Evaluate strengths, weaknesses, limitations, and next steps.
- Reflect on citizenship skills used.

Locations:

- Research Lab.
- Survey Street.
- Planning Board.
- Action Stage.
- Impact Observatory.
- Reflection Room.

NPCs:

- Councillor Noor: project planning.
- Surveyor Tess: questionnaires and interviews.
- Statistician Jules: charts and evidence.
- Event Organiser Kai: action methods.
- Examiner Mira: evaluation and long-answer structure.

Quest Examples:

- Design a fair survey question.
- Collect evidence from three viewpoints.
- Choose an action method for a real local issue.
- Build an impact report using before/after evidence.
- Write an evaluation that includes limitations and improvements.

Exam Skills:

- Describe research methods.
- Explain why evidence is reliable.
- Evaluate impact.
- Write balanced conclusions.
- Connect action to citizenship concepts.

## Exam Hall

Role: revision and assessment training region unlocked gradually.

Core Topics:

- Command words: identify, describe, explain, analyse, evaluate.
- Source usefulness and reliability.
- Balanced argument.
- Using examples.
- Structuring 4, 6, 8, and 12 mark answers.
- Timed practice.

Locations:

- Command Word Corridor.
- Source Analysis Desk.
- Paragraph Forge.
- Debate Arena.
- Mock Paper Gate.

NPCs:

- Examiner Mira: mark scheme logic.
- Timekeeper Ash: timed practice.
- Source Keeper Nia: source questions.
- Debate Coach Leon: balanced judgement.

Quest Examples:

- Upgrade a weak paragraph into a stronger one.
- Choose the best evidence for a claim.
- Spot missing balance in a long answer.
- Build a PEEL paragraph.
- Complete a timed boss question.

Rewards:

- Grade tokens.
- Answer frames.
- Better equipment skins.
- Region mastery badges.

## Recommended Game Progression

1. Citizenship Village: tutorial and core concepts.
2. Modern Britain Borough: identity, media, society, UK in the world.
3. Rights & Law Quarter: rights, duties, law, justice.
4. Democracy Capital: Parliament, government, elections, power.
5. Participation Harbour: campaigns, pressure groups, public voice.
6. Action Workshop: active citizenship project.
7. Exam Hall: integrated revision and exam practice.

## Design Notes

- Each region should contain story quests, retrieval-practice mini-quests, and exam-skill challenges.
- Every major GCSE concept should be taught through an action: interview, investigate, compare, judge, campaign, or evaluate.
- Rewards should reflect learning: civic clothing, evidence tools, debate tools, source lenses, campaign banners, and exam charms.
- The final game should allow free revisiting of regions for spaced retrieval practice.
